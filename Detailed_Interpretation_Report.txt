================================================================================
1. INTRODUCTION AND STUDY OVERVIEW
================================================================================

This study conducted a two-phase investigation of the human tumor microbiome,
leveraging publicly available data from the landmark Nejman et al. (2020)
study published in Science. The original Nejman study profiled 1,526 tumors
and their adjacent normal tissues across seven cancer types (breast, lung,
ovary, pancreas, melanoma, bone, and brain), demonstrating that intracellular
bacteria are present within tumor cells and immune cells in the tumor
microenvironment, and that each tumor type harbors a distinct microbiome
composition. Their work established that breast cancer tumors have a
particularly rich and diverse microbiome compared to other cancer types.

From this original dataset, the present study focused on three cancer types --
breast, lung, and ovary -- specifically because these were the cancer types
for which both normal adjacent tissue (NAT) and tumor samples were available
from paired patients. Unpaired patient data (where only NAT or only tumor
samples were available) were excluded, as many differential abundance (DA)
tools do not account for the unpaired design, and including such data could
introduce confounding effects.

The dataset consisted of:
  - Breast: 308 samples (154 NAT + 154 Tumor, paired)
  - Lung: 456 samples (228 NAT + 228 Tumor, paired)
  - Ovary: 24 samples (12 NAT + 12 Tumor, paired)

The study comprised two main phases:

PHASE 1: Differential Abundance Analysis
  - Four DA tools: DESeq2, ANCOM-BC2, ALDEx2, and MaAsLin2
  - Two imputation methods: BMDD and mbImpute
  - Two prevalence filtering thresholds: 5% and 10%
  - Three analysis conditions per cancer type:
    (a) Raw data with 5% and 10% filtering
    (b) mbImpute-imputed data with 5% and 10% filtering
    (c) BMDD-imputed data with 5% and 10% filtering

PHASE 2: Machine Learning Classification
  - Balanced Random Forest and XGBoost classifiers
  - Tumor type classification across five cancer types (breast, lung,
    melanoma, ovary, pancreas)
  - Stratified 5-fold cross-validation
  - SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) for class imbalance
  - 901 total tumor samples for classification

================================================================================
2. METHODOLOGY: TOOLS AND APPROACHES
================================================================================

2.1 Differential Abundance Tools
---------------------------------

2.1.1 DESeq2
Reference: Love MI, Huber W, Anders S. "Moderated estimation of fold change
and dispersion for RNA-seq data with DESeq2." Genome Biology. 2014;15(12):550.

DESeq2 was originally developed for RNA-seq differential expression analysis
and models count data using a negative binomial distribution. It performs
library size normalization using median-of-ratios, estimates gene-wise
dispersions using empirical Bayes shrinkage, and tests for differential
abundance using a Wald test. For microbiome data, DESeq2 is known to be
liberal (prone to higher false positive rates) particularly with sparse,
zero-inflated datasets, as documented by Weiss et al. (2017) and Nearing
et al. (2022). This is reflected in our results, where DESeq2 consistently
identified the largest number of significant genera across all cancer types
and conditions. DESeq2 does not inherently address the compositional nature
of microbiome data, which is a known limitation when applied to 16S rRNA
or metagenomic abundance tables.

2.1.2 ANCOM-BC2
Reference: Lin H, Peddada SD. "Analysis of compositions of microbiomes with
bias correction." Nature Communications. 2020;11:3514.
Updated: Lin H, Peddada SD. "ANCOM-BC2: Analysis of compositions of
microbiomes with bias correction 2." 2023.

ANCOM-BC2 explicitly addresses the compositionality problem in microbiome
data by estimating and correcting for the unknown sampling fraction (the
ratio of observed counts to absolute abundances). It provides both standard
("diff") and robust ("diff_robust") significance assessments. The robust
test applies an additional sensitivity analysis that accounts for the
possibility that the reference frame (used for bias correction) may itself
be differentially abundant. In our results, ANCOM-BC2 findings were
significant under the standard test (diff = TRUE) but not under the robust
test (diff_robust = FALSE, direction_robust = "ns") across most findings.
This indicates that while there is statistical signal for differential
abundance, the findings are sensitive to the choice of reference taxa --
a common outcome in low-biomass microbiome studies where many taxa may be
differentially abundant simultaneously, violating the assumption that most
taxa are unchanged.

2.1.3 ALDEx2
Reference: Fernandes AD, Reid JN, Macklaim JM, McMurrough TA, Edgell DR,
Gloor GB. "Unifying the analysis of high-throughput sequencing datasets:
characterizing RNA-seq, 16S rRNA gene sequencing and selective growth
experiments by compositional data analysis." Microbiome. 2014;2:15.

ALDEx2 uses a compositionally aware approach based on the centered
log-ratio (CLR) transformation. It generates Monte Carlo instances of the
Dirichlet distribution to estimate technical variation arising from
sequencing, applies the CLR transformation to each instance, and then
uses Welch's t-test or Wilcoxon rank-sum test to assess differential
abundance. The effect size reported by ALDEx2 represents the median
difference between conditions divided by the median within-condition
difference, making it robust to outliers. ALDEx2 is widely regarded as
the most conservative of the four tools (Nearing et al. 2022), which
explains why it identified the fewest significant genera in our analyses.
The overlap statistic (values near 0.4-0.5 in our results) indicates
substantial distributional overlap between groups, consistent with small
but detectable effect sizes.

2.1.4 MaAsLin2
Reference: Mallick H, Rahnavard A, McIver LJ, et al. "Multivariable
association discovery in population-scale meta-omics studies." PLoS
Computational Biology. 2021;17(11):e1009442.

MaAsLin2 (Microbiome Multivariable Associations with Linear Models) fits
per-feature (per-taxon) generalized linear models, allowing incorporation
of multiple covariates and random effects. By default, it applies
total-sum scaling (TSS) normalization and log transformation, then fits
linear models to identify associations. MaAsLin2 identified a moderate
number of significant genera in our analyses, generally more than ALDEx2
but fewer than DESeq2.

2.2 Imputation Methods
-----------------------

2.2.1 BMDD (Bayesian Model for the Detection of Differentially abundant
taxa with zero-inflation)
Reference: Zhou H, Zhang K. GitHub: https://github.com/zhouhj1994/BMDD

BMDD addresses the zero-inflation problem in microbiome count data using
a Bayesian framework. It models observed zeros as arising from two sources:
structural zeros (true biological absence) and sampling zeros (taxa present
but not detected due to insufficient sequencing depth). BMDD imputes
sampling zeros with estimated count values based on the posterior
distribution, thereby potentially recovering true biological signal
obscured by undersampling. Note: BMDD could not be applied to the ovary
dataset due to the very small sample size (n=24), as the Bayesian model
requires sufficient data for stable posterior estimation.

2.2.2 mbImpute
Reference: Jiang R, Li WV, Li JJ. "mbImpute: an accurate and robust
imputation method for microbiome data." Genome Biology. 2021;22:192.
GitHub: https://github.com/ruochenj/mbImpute

mbImpute imputes likely false zeros in microbiome data using a
regularized regression framework that borrows information across samples
and taxa. It uses sample covariates (metadata) and taxon-taxon
correlations to predict whether a zero is structural or a sampling artifact,
then imputes sampling zeros with estimated values. mbImpute differs from
BMDD in that it uses a frequentist, regression-based approach rather than
a Bayesian model.

2.3 Prevalence Filtering
--------------------------

Prevalence filtering at 5% and 10% thresholds removes taxa that appear
in fewer than 5% or 10% of samples, respectively. This is standard
practice in microbiome studies (recommended in workflows such as those by
Callahan et al. 2016, and the QIIME2/phyloseq documentation) to:

  (a) Remove taxa that may represent sequencing artifacts or contaminants
  (b) Reduce the multiple testing burden
  (c) Focus on taxa with sufficient prevalence to make meaningful
      statistical inferences
  (d) Reduce noise from rare taxa that inflate zero-inflation

The 10% filter is more stringent, retaining fewer taxa. In our analyses,
the 10% filter generally resulted in fewer significant findings but with
potentially higher confidence, as the remaining taxa had more consistent
presence across samples.

2.4 Tool Agreement and Benchmarking Context
---------------------------------------------

Nearing et al. (2022, Nature Communications) conducted a comprehensive
benchmarking of DA methods across 38 microbiome datasets and found that
different tools often produce highly discordant results, with average
overlap between tools as low as ~20-30%. They found that ALDEx2 and
ANCOM-BC were among the most conservative methods, while DESeq2 tended
to identify many more features, often including false positives.
Similarly, Wallen (2021) demonstrated poor concordance among DA tools
when applied to the same microbiome datasets. This poor inter-tool
agreement is an important interpretive framework for understanding the
results of the present study.

3. PHASE 1 RESULTS: DIFFERENTIAL ABUNDANCE ANALYSIS
================================================================================

3.1 BREAST DATA ANALYSIS
--------------------------

The breast dataset (n=308, 154 paired samples) yielded the most findings
across all tools and conditions, consistent with Nejman et al.'s
observation that breast tumors harbor the most diverse microbiome among
the cancer types studied.

3.1.1 Number of Significant Genera by Tool and Condition (Breast):

Condition                  | DESeq2 | ALDEx2 | MaAsLin2 | ANCOM-BC2
---------------------------|--------|--------|----------|----------
Raw 5% filter              |   51   |   1    |    2     |    0
Raw 10% filter             |   26   |   0    |    0     |    0
BMDD 5% filter             |    1*  |   2    |    1     |    0
BMDD 10% filter            |    0   |   7    |    2     |    0
mbImpute 5% filter (Aldex) |    ~55 |   1    |    0     |    3
mbImpute 5% filter (Deseq) |   ~55  |   --   |    --    |    --
mbImpute 10% filter        |   ~23  |   1    |    6     |    1

(*Some mbImpute DESeq2 results extend to many genera; counts estimated
from the data provided)

KEY OBSERVATION - Staphylococcus as a Consistently Significant Genus:

The most striking finding in the breast data is the consistent
identification of Staphylococcus as tumor-enriched across nearly all tools
and conditions:

  - ALDEx2: Significant in raw 5% (q=0.037), BMDD 5% (q=0.018),
    BMDD 10% (q=0.021), mbImpute 5% (q=0.037), mbImpute 10% (q=0.035)
  - DESeq2: Significant in raw 5% (padj=0.001), raw 10% (padj=0.001)
  - MaAsLin2: Significant in raw 5% (qval=0.040), BMDD 5% (qval=0.045),
    BMDD 10% (qval=0.026), mbImpute 5% (qval=0.029), mbImpute 10%
    (qval=0.015)
  - ANCOM-BC2: Not significant for Staphylococcus specifically

This cross-tool consistency is notable given the general poor concordance
among DA methods documented by Nearing et al. (2022). The finding is also
biologically validated: Urbaniak et al. (2016, Applied and Environmental
Microbiology) demonstrated that Staphylococcus was significantly more
abundant in breast tumor tissue compared to paired normal tissue from the
same patients. Furthermore, Hieken et al. (2016, Scientific Reports) found
that the breast tumor microbiome was enriched in Staphylococcus species.
The mechanism may involve Staphylococcus's capacity to produce DNA
double-strand breaks via toxins such as cytolethal distending toxin (CDT)
analogs, potentially contributing to genomic instability, although this
remains speculative for breast tissue specifically.

Staphylococcus is also a known skin commensal, and breast tissue is
anatomically proximate to skin. However, Nejman et al. (2020) employed
extensive contamination controls including paraffin-only controls, DNA
extraction controls, and PCR negative controls, and confirmed intracellular
localization of bacteria via fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH).
Still, the possibility of skin contamination during surgical procedures
cannot be entirely excluded and should be considered as a caveat (Salter
et al. 2014, BMC Biology).

KEY OBSERVATION - Prevotella:

Prevotella was identified as tumor-enriched in breast tissue by:
  - MaAsLin2 (raw 5%: qval=0.040)
  - ALDEx2 (BMDD 5%: q=0.031, BMDD 10%: q=0.035)
  - MaAsLin2 (mbImpute 5%: qval=0.040, mbImpute 10%: qval=0.030)

Prevotella is primarily an oral/gut anaerobe not typically associated with
breast tissue in healthy individuals. Its enrichment in tumors could
reflect the altered tumor microenvironment (lower oxygen, altered immune
surveillance, different metabolite availability) that may favor anaerobic
organisms. This finding is consistent with the concept that the tumor
microenvironment selects for specific bacterial communities, as proposed
by Nejman et al. (2020). Parhi et al. (2020, Cancer Discovery) have also
documented Prevotella associations with colorectal cancer.

KEY OBSERVATION - NAT-enriched genera:

Across conditions, several genera were consistently NAT-enriched
(depleted in tumors):
  - Afipia: Identified by DESeq2 across raw and imputed conditions
  - Sphaerobacter: Consistent NAT enrichment via DESeq2
  - Comamonas, Sphingobacterium, Citrobacter: Repeatedly NAT-enriched
  - Kocuria: NAT-enriched across conditions

The NAT enrichment of these genera may reflect the disruption of the
normal tissue microbiome during tumorigenesis, where the tumor
microenvironment selectively promotes certain taxa while suppressing
others.

KEY OBSERVATION - DESeq2 Liberal Detection:

DESeq2 identified 51 significant genera in the raw 5% filter condition
alone, compared to 1 for ALDEx2 and 2 for MaAsLin2. This dramatic
disparity is consistent with the known liberal behavior of DESeq2 in
microbiome settings (Weiss et al. 2017, Microbiome; Nearing et al. 2022).
The negative binomial model of DESeq2, while appropriate for RNA-seq
data, may overfit to the excess zeros and overdispersion common in
microbiome data, leading to inflated false positive rates.

KEY OBSERVATION - Effect of Imputation:

Imputation with BMDD and mbImpute had variable effects:
  - With BMDD 5% filter: ALDEx2 detected 2 genera (Staphylococcus and
    Prevotella), up from 1 in raw data. This suggests BMDD helped recover
    signal for Prevotella that was masked by zeros.
  - With BMDD 10% filter: ALDEx2 detected 7 genera (including Klebsiella,
    Afipia, Haemophilus, Janibacter, Sphaerobacter), substantially more
    than in any raw data condition. This may indicate that BMDD imputation
    at the 10% filter level reduced zero-inflation sufficiently for ALDEx2
    to detect additional signals, but also raises concerns about potential
    false positives introduced by imputation.
  - mbImpute generally produced results more similar to raw data for the
    conservative tools (ALDEx2 remained at 1 significant genus in most
    conditions), suggesting it preserved the data structure more
    conservatively.
  - ANCOM-BC2 only produced results with mbImpute data, suggesting that
    mbImpute's imputation may have improved the data properties needed
    for ANCOM-BC2's bias correction to work effectively.

3.1.2 ANCOM-BC2 Robust vs. Non-Robust Results:

All ANCOM-BC2 results in the breast data were significant under the standard
test (diff = TRUE) but not under the robust test (diff_robust = FALSE).
This means the findings were sensitive to the assumption that the reference
taxa used for bias correction are not themselves differentially abundant.
In tumor microbiome studies where the entire microbial community may be
altered, this assumption is commonly violated, explaining the lack of
robust significance.

3.2 LUNG DATA ANALYSIS
------------------------

The lung dataset (n=456, 228 paired samples) had the largest sample size
and yielded extensive findings, particularly from DESeq2.

3.2.1 Number of Significant Genera by Tool and Condition (Lung):

Condition                  | DESeq2 | ALDEx2 | MaAsLin2 | ANCOM-BC2
---------------------------|--------|--------|----------|----------
Raw 5% filter              |   71   |   0    |    0     |    0
Raw 10% filter             |   29   |   0    |    2     |    0
BMDD 5% filter             |   ~75  |   2    |    1     |    0
BMDD 10% filter            |    0   |   0    |    0     |    0
mbImpute 5% filter         |   ~75  |   0    |    0     |    3
mbImpute 10% filter        |   ~26  |   0    |    3     |    0

KEY OBSERVATION - Highly Asymmetric Tool Sensitivity:

The lung data exhibits the most extreme disparity between tools. DESeq2
identified 71 significant genera in the raw 5% filter condition, while
ALDEx2, MaAsLin2, and ANCOM-BC2 found zero. This is a stark illustration
of the benchmarking findings by Nearing et al. (2022) and suggests that
the lung microbiome differences between NAT and tumor, while detectable
by the liberal DESeq2 model, are of insufficient effect size to pass the
more stringent statistical thresholds of compositionally-aware methods.

KEY OBSERVATION - Bacillus as NAT-enriched (depleted in tumors):

Among the few genera consistently identified across tools and conditions:
  - Bacillus: NAT-enriched by DESeq2 (raw 5%: padj=7.23E-28; raw 10%:
    padj=1.94E-26) and MaAsLin2 (raw 10%: qval=0.038; mbImpute 5%:
    qval=0.044; mbImpute 10%: qval=0.025). Bacillus is a spore-forming
    environmental bacterium. Its depletion in lung tumors compared to NAT
    may reflect altered mucosal immunity in the tumor microenvironment or
    competitive exclusion by tumor-associated bacteria.

  - Devosia: NAT-enriched by DESeq2 and MaAsLin2 across multiple
    conditions. Devosia is a soil/environmental alphaproteobacterium with
    no well-established role in human tissue, and its presence in both
    NAT and tumor samples at low biomass may reflect environmental
    contamination or transient colonization.

KEY OBSERVATION - Friedmanniella (BMDD-specific finding):

With BMDD imputation and 5% filter, ALDEx2 identified Friedmanniella as
tumor-enriched (q=0.025), and MaAsLin2 confirmed this (qval=0.044). This
genus was NOT identified in any raw data analysis. Friedmanniella is an
actinobacterium found in environmental samples. This finding emerged only
after imputation, suggesting either: (a) BMDD successfully recovered a
genuine biological signal that was obscured by zeros, or (b) the
imputation introduced artifacts. The fact that two independent tools
confirmed the finding lends some support to interpretation (a), but it
should be validated with additional experiments.

KEY OBSERVATION - BMDD 10% filter yielded zero results for all tools:

This is notable because it suggests that at the more stringent filtering
threshold, BMDD imputation for lung data did not produce sufficient
signal for any tool to detect differential abundance. This could indicate
that the lung microbiome differences at the genus level are subtle and
easily lost when rare taxa are removed.

KEY OBSERVATION - Many DESeq2 genera in lung showed strong directionality:

In the raw 5% DESeq2 results, many more genera were NAT-enriched than
tumor-enriched. Key NAT-enriched genera included: Ferruginibacter,
Bacillus, Aeromonas, Bacteroides, Brachybacterium, Lactococcus,
Leuconostoc, Hydrogenophaga, Halomonas, Arthrobacter, and Veillonella.
Key tumor-enriched genera included: Skermanella, Friedmanniella,
Caulobacter, Kaistobacter, Flavobacterium, and Stenotrophomonas.

The predominance of NAT-enriched genera suggests that, in the lung, the
normal adjacent tissue may harbor a richer commensal microbiome that is
disrupted or displaced during tumorigenesis. This is consistent with the
lung being an organ with substantial environmental microbial exposure
through respiration.

3.3 OVARY DATA ANALYSIS
-------------------------

The ovary dataset (n=24, 12 paired samples) had the smallest sample size,
severely limiting statistical power.

3.3.1 Number of Significant Genera by Tool and Condition (Ovary):

Condition                  | DESeq2 | ALDEx2 | MaAsLin2 | ANCOM-BC2
---------------------------|--------|--------|----------|----------
Raw 5% filter              |    9   |   0    |    0     |    0
Raw 10% filter             |    7   |   0    |    0     |    1
mbImpute 5% filter         |    5   |   0    |    0     |    4
mbImpute 10% filter        |    4   |   0    |    0     |    4
BMDD: Not applicable (sample size too small)

KEY OBSERVATION - Limited Power Due to Small Sample Size:

The ovary dataset's extremely small sample size (n=24) severely limited
the ability of any tool to detect differential abundance. ALDEx2 and
MaAsLin2 found zero significant genera across ALL conditions, reflecting
their higher statistical stringency combined with insufficient sample
size. Even DESeq2, the most liberal tool, identified at most 9 genera.

KEY OBSERVATION - Staphylococcus and Escherichia/Shigella in Ovary:

Despite the small sample size, two genera showed consistent tumor
enrichment:
  - Staphylococcus: Tumor-enriched by DESeq2 (raw 5%: padj=0.030;
    raw 10%: padj=0.017; mbImpute 5%: padj=0.021; mbImpute 10%:
    padj=0.003) and ANCOM-BC2 (mbImpute 5%: q=3.13E-08; mbImpute 10%:
    q=3.66E-07). The consistency of Staphylococcus tumor enrichment
    across breast AND ovary datasets is noteworthy.

  - Escherichia/Shigella: Tumor-enriched by DESeq2 (raw 5%: padj=0.013;
    raw 10%: padj=0.004) and ANCOM-BC2 (mbImpute 5%: q=4.83E-05;
    mbImpute 10%: q=7.76E-06). However, a contradictory result appeared
    in ANCOM-BC2 with raw 10% filter, where Escherichia/Shigella was
    called NAT-enriched (q=0.040). This discrepancy highlights the
    instability of results in small samples and the impact of imputation
    on directionality.

KEY OBSERVATION - Pseudomonas dominance:

Pseudomonas had extremely high baseMean values in the ovary DESeq2 results
(baseMean = 3437 in raw 5%, 4061 in raw 10%), orders of magnitude higher
than any other genus. Pseudomonas was consistently NAT-enriched. This
extreme abundance raises contamination concerns, as Pseudomonas is one of
the most commonly reported contaminant genera in low-biomass microbiome
studies (Salter et al. 2014, BMC Biology; Eisenhofer et al. 2019, Trends
in Microbiology). However, the paired study design (same patient NAT vs.
tumor) partially mitigates this concern, as contamination would be expected
to affect both sample types equally.

KEY OBSERVATION - ANCOM-BC2 showed activity with mbImpute:

ANCOM-BC2 produced results only with mbImpute-imputed data in the ovary
dataset. With raw data, ANCOM-BC2 found at most 1 genus. With mbImpute,
it identified 4 genera consistently across 5% and 10% filters
(Staphylococcus, Escherichia/Shigella, Leuconostoc, Unknown genus41).
This suggests mbImpute may have improved data properties sufficiently
for ANCOM-BC2's linear model and bias correction to converge.

Importantly, all ANCOM-BC2 ovary results were significant under the
standard test but NOT under the robust test (diff_robust = FALSE),
consistent with the breast data pattern.

3.4 Cross-Cancer Comparison Summary
--------------------------------------

3.4.1 Tool Sensitivity Ranking (consistent across all cancer types):

  DESeq2 >> MaAsLin2 > ALDEx2 > ANCOM-BC2 (in terms of number of
  significant genera detected)

This ranking is consistent with the benchmarking literature (Nearing
et al. 2022). The high detection rate of DESeq2 likely includes both
true and false positives, while ALDEx2 and ANCOM-BC2's conservative
nature may miss true positives but provides higher confidence in the
genera they do identify.

3.4.2 Genera Showing Cross-Cancer Consistency:

  - Staphylococcus: Tumor-enriched in both breast and ovary across
    multiple tools and conditions. This is the single most robust
    finding of the study. This cross-cancer tumor enrichment is
    consistent with Nejman et al. (2020), who noted the broad presence
    of Staphylococcus across tumor types, and with Urbaniak et al.
    (2016) for breast specifically.

  - Pseudomonas: Present at high abundance in ovary data; one of the
    top features in classification models. Its role requires careful
    interpretation given contamination concerns.

3.4.3 Effect of Sample Size:

The dramatic difference in findings between breast/lung (large samples)
and ovary (small sample) underscores the critical importance of sample
size in microbiome studies. The ovary dataset's 24 samples are well
below the recommended minimum for most DA tools (typically n > 50-100
for adequate power; Weiss et al. 2017).

4. PHASE 2 RESULTS: MACHINE LEARNING CLASSIFICATION
================================================================================

The second phase attempted to classify tumor samples by cancer type
(breast, lung, melanoma, ovary, pancreas) using their microbiome
profiles. The total dataset comprised 901 tumor samples:
  - Breast: 338 (37.5%)
  - Lung: 243 (27.0%)
  - Melanoma: 197 (21.9%)
  - Ovary: 56 (6.2%)
  - Pancreas: 67 (7.4%)

4.2 Interpretation of Classification Performance
---------------------------------------------------

4.2.1 Overall Accuracy Context:

The accuracies of 59-63% are modest but meaningful in context. Random
chance classification across 5 classes would yield ~20% accuracy (or
~37.5% if always predicting the majority class). The achieved accuracies
represent a substantial improvement over random chance, suggesting that
microbial community composition does carry discriminatory information
for cancer type classification.

For comparison, Poore et al. (2020, Nature) achieved higher accuracies
(~65-80%) for cancer type discrimination using microbiome data, but their
study used a much larger dataset (>18,000 samples from TCGA), more
sophisticated feature engineering, and a different analytical pipeline.
Our lower accuracy is expected given the smaller sample size and simpler
approach.

4.2.2 Per-Class Performance Analysis:

BREAST (Best classified: F1 = 0.71-0.73):
Breast tumors were the most accurately classified, with precision of
0.64-0.75 and recall of 0.71-0.82. This is consistent with Nejman et
al.'s finding that breast tumors have the most diverse and distinctive
microbiome, providing more discriminatory features. The confusion
matrices show breast samples were primarily confused with lung (52-66
misclassifications), which may reflect shared environmental microbial
exposures or similar tissue characteristics.

MELANOMA (Second best: F1 = 0.64-0.70):
Melanoma showed good classification, with precision of 0.59-0.77 and
recall of 0.64-0.69. The baseline RF (without SMOTE) achieved the
highest melanoma F1 of 0.70 with precision of 0.77. Melanoma's
distinctive skin-associated microbiome likely provides discriminatory
features not shared with internal organ tumors.

LUNG (Moderate: F1 = 0.49-0.53):
Lung classification was moderate, with substantial confusion with breast
(43-88 misclassifications) and melanoma (18-45). The lung microbiome
may share features with both breast (anatomical proximity, mucosal
surfaces) and melanoma (environmental exposure), reducing discriminatory
power.

PANCREAS (Moderate: F1 = 0.48-0.54):
Despite having only 67 samples, pancreas showed moderate classification
with recall of 0.40-0.48. SMOTE appeared to improve pancreas recall
compared to the baseline RF.

OVARY (Poor: F1 = 0.00-0.09):
Ovary classification was essentially failed across all models. The
baseline RF achieved 0 precision, 0 recall, and 0 F1 for ovary. Even
with SMOTE, the best F1 was 0.09 (RF improved) and 0.09 (XGBoost).
The confusion matrices reveal the problem clearly:
  - Baseline RF: All 56 ovary samples were misclassified (25 as breast,
    22 as lung, 9 as melanoma)
  - RF with SMOTE: Only 3 of 56 correctly classified (17 as breast,
    19 as lung, 16 as melanoma, 1 as pancreas)
  - XGBoost with SMOTE: Only 4 of 56 correctly classified

This failure has multiple contributing factors:
  (a) Extreme class imbalance: Ovary (56 samples, 6.2%) vs. breast
      (338, 37.5%). Even with SMOTE, the synthetic samples may not
      capture the true distributional properties of the ovary microbiome.
  (b) Small original sample size: With only 56 samples in 5-fold CV,
      each fold has only ~11 ovary test samples, providing insufficient
      data for the model to learn ovary-specific patterns.
  (c) Possible biological overlap: The ovary microbiome may genuinely
      lack distinctive features that separate it from other cancer types,
      or the low bacterial biomass in ovarian tumors may result in a
      microbiome profile dominated by noise rather than signal.

4.2.3 Effect of SMOTE:

Comparing baseline RF (no SMOTE) to RF with SMOTE:
  - SMOTE slightly reduced overall accuracy (0.628 -> 0.613)
  - SMOTE improved ovary recall from 0.00 to 0.05 and F1 from 0.00 to
    0.09 -- still very poor, but no longer zero
  - SMOTE improved pancreas recall from 0.40 to 0.48
  - SMOTE reduced melanoma performance (F1 from 0.70 to 0.64)

This trade-off is characteristic of SMOTE: by oversampling minority
classes, the model sacrifices majority class performance for minority
class gains. However, the gains for ovary were minimal, suggesting that
SMOTE cannot overcome the fundamental lack of discriminatory signal in
the ovary microbiome data. Chawla et al. (2002) originally demonstrated
SMOTE's limitations with very small minority classes and high-dimensional
data, both of which apply here.

4.2.4 RF vs. XGBoost:

Random Forest slightly outperformed XGBoost across all metrics:
  - Mean accuracy: RF 0.613 vs. XGBoost 0.592
  - Weighted F1: RF 0.60 vs. XGBoost 0.59
  - Per-class: RF matched or exceeded XGBoost in all categories

This may reflect Random Forest's better handling of noise in
high-dimensional, low-signal microbiome data, where individual trees'
bagging approach provides more robust variance reduction. XGBoost's
sequential boosting may overfit to noise in this setting.

4.3.2 Interpretation of Feature Importances:

CONSISTENT TOP FEATURES ACROSS MODELS:
Several genera appear in the top 10 across multiple models:
  - Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Pseudomonas, Staphylococcus,
    Escherichia/Shigella, and Corynebacterium

These are all common human-associated or environmental bacteria. Their
importance for classification does not necessarily indicate a causal
role in cancer but rather that their abundance patterns differ across
tumor types in a way the classifier can exploit.

CONTAMINATION CONSIDERATIONS:
Several top features (Pseudomonas, Escherichia/Shigella, Klebsiella,
Enterobacter, Acinetobacter) are known potential contaminants in
low-biomass sequencing studies (Salter et al. 2014; Eisenhofer et al.
2019). While Nejman et al. (2020) implemented contamination controls,
the prominence of these genera in classification features warrants
caution. If some of these signals derive partly from contamination
patterns that differ by tissue type or processing batch, the
classifier may be partially learning technical rather than biological
variation.

BIOLOGICALLY PLAUSIBLE FEATURES:
  - Corynebacterium: A skin-associated genus whose differential
    abundance across tumor types is biologically plausible (skin tumors
    like melanoma would have different levels than internal tumors).
  - Streptococcus: An oral/respiratory bacterium whose differential
    abundance between lung and other tumor types is expected.
  - Staphylococcus: Consistently identified as tumor-enriched in DA
    analysis and present in top classification features, providing
    convergent evidence for its relevance.
  - Rothia: An oral commensal found in top features of SMOTE models;
    Rothia has been associated with lung cancer in some studies.

4.3.3 Feature Importance Distribution:

The feature importance values are notably flat. In the baseline RF, the
top feature (Pseudomonas) has importance of 0.024, and importances
decrease gradually with many genera contributing small amounts. This
flat distribution indicates that no single genus is strongly
discriminatory; rather, classification relies on the collective pattern
of many genera with weak individual signals. This is consistent with
the concept that the tumor microbiome is characterized by community-level
shifts rather than the dominance or absence of individual taxa.

The total number of features in the baseline RF was >1,600 genera, of
which many had zero or near-zero importance. The improved models used
78 genera after low-variance filtering, which is a reasonable reduction
that removes uninformative features while retaining enough for pattern
detection.

================================================================================
5. DISCUSSION
================================================================================

5.1 Key Findings and Their Significance
------------------------------------------

This study's most robust finding is the consistent tumor enrichment of
Staphylococcus in breast tissue, identified by all four DA tools across
multiple preprocessing conditions. This finding aligns with prior
literature (Urbaniak et al. 2016; Hieken et al. 2016; Nejman et al. 2020)
and represents the strongest signal in the differential abundance analysis.
The convergence of multiple methodologically distinct tools (from the
liberal DESeq2 to the conservative ALDEx2) on this result provides
confidence that it reflects genuine biological signal rather than a
statistical artifact of any single method.

The second notable finding is the dramatic difference in tool sensitivity,
with DESeq2 identifying 10-100x more significant genera than other tools.
This is not a novel observation -- it is well-documented in the
benchmarking literature (Nearing et al. 2022) -- but its demonstration
in the specific context of tumor microbiome data is valuable. It
emphasizes that results from any single DA tool should be interpreted
cautiously, and that multi-tool consensus approaches (as employed in
this study) provide a more reliable picture of true differential
abundance.

5.2 The Imputation Question
------------------------------

The variable effects of BMDD and mbImpute imputation across cancer types
and tools highlight the unresolved question of how best to handle zeros
in microbiome data. In some cases (e.g., Prevotella in breast with BMDD),
imputation appeared to reveal signal masked by zeros. In other cases
(e.g., lung with BMDD 10% filter), imputation produced no significant
results. The fact that BMDD could not be applied to the ovary data due
to small sample size further illustrates the practical limitations of
imputation approaches.

A conservative interpretation is that findings consistent between raw
and imputed data (e.g., Staphylococcus in breast) are more reliable
than findings that emerge only after imputation (e.g., Friedmanniella
in lung with BMDD). The latter should be considered hypothesis-generating
rather than confirmatory.

5.3 Limitations
-----------------

5.3.1 Sample Size Disparities:
The ovary dataset (n=24) is too small for reliable statistical inference.
Both the DA analysis and machine learning classification suffered from
this limitation. Future studies should prioritize larger ovarian tumor
cohorts.

5.3.2 Contamination Concerns:
Low-biomass microbiome studies are inherently susceptible to contamination
(Salter et al. 2014; Eisenhofer et al. 2019). While Nejman et al. (2020)
implemented extensive controls, recent work by Gihawi et al. (2023,
Nature) has questioned the validity of some tumor microbiome findings,
suggesting that batch effects and contamination may account for a
substantial portion of reported signals. This caveat applies to all
findings in the present study.

5.3.3 Compositionality:
Microbiome sequencing data are inherently compositional (Gloor et al.
2017, Frontiers in Microbiology). While ALDEx2 and ANCOM-BC2 explicitly
address this, DESeq2 and MaAsLin2 do not fully account for
compositionality, which may contribute to their higher detection rates.

5.3.4 Multiple Testing:
With four tools, two imputation methods, two filter thresholds, and
three cancer types, the total number of comparisons is large. While
each tool applies its own multiple testing correction (Benjamini-Hochberg
for DESeq2, ALDEx2, and MaAsLin2; a similar procedure for ANCOM-BC2),
there is no correction across the meta-analytical framework of combining
tools and conditions. This inflates the overall false discovery rate.

5.3.5 Paired Design Not Fully Leveraged:
The study used paired patient data (NAT and tumor from the same
individual), but not all DA tools can account for paired designs.
DESeq2 and MaAsLin2 can include patient as a random effect or blocking
factor, while ALDEx2's standard two-group tests do not. It is unclear
from the provided data whether the paired structure was utilized where
possible, which could affect sensitivity and specificity.

5.3.6 Classification Limitations:
The classification task used only genus-level abundance data without
additional clinical or molecular features. Integration of multi-omic
data (gene expression, mutations, methylation) with microbiome data
could potentially improve classification performance, as demonstrated
by Poore et al. (2020).

5.4 Comparison with the Literature
-------------------------------------

The findings of this study are broadly consistent with the existing
tumor microbiome literature:

  - Nejman et al. (2020) reported tumor type-specific microbiomes,
    which our classification results support (accuracy above chance).
  - The breast tumor microbiome being the most distinctive and diverse
    (most DA findings, best classification) aligns with Nejman et al.
    and subsequent studies.
  - Staphylococcus enrichment in breast tumors is supported by
    Urbaniak et al. (2016), Hieken et al. (2016), and Thompson et al.
    (2017).
  - The poor concordance between DA tools is consistent with Nearing
    et al. (2022) and Wallen (2021).
  - The modest classification accuracy (~60-63%) is within the expected
    range for microbiome-based cancer classification with limited
    sample sizes.

================================================================================
6. CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

This study provides a systematic evaluation of differential abundance
analysis tools, imputation methods, and machine learning classifiers
applied to the human tumor microbiome. The key conclusions are:

1. Staphylococcus is robustly identified as tumor-enriched in breast
   tissue across all four DA tools and both imputation methods. This is
   the most reliable finding and is supported by independent literature.

2. DA tools vary dramatically in sensitivity, with DESeq2 being 10-100x
   more liberal than ALDEx2 or ANCOM-BC2. Multi-tool consensus approaches
   are essential for reliable inference.

3. Imputation (BMDD and mbImpute) has context-dependent effects: sometimes
   revealing signal, sometimes producing no change, and potentially
   introducing artifacts. Findings unique to imputed data should be
   treated as hypothesis-generating.

4. Machine learning classification of tumor types from microbiome data
   achieves modest but above-chance accuracy (~60-63%), with breast and
   melanoma being the most classifiable and ovary being essentially
   unclassifiable with the available data.

5. Ovary data (n=24) is severely underpowered for both DA analysis and
   machine learning classification, yielding unreliable results.

6. Feature importance analysis identifies both biologically plausible
   genera (Staphylococcus, Corynebacterium, Streptococcus) and potential
   contaminants (Pseudomonas, Klebsiella) as top discriminators,
   warranting cautious interpretation.
